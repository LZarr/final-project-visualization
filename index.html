Concentric Zone Model

Park & Burgess, 1925

The Chicago School proposed that cities grow outward in concentric rings: a central business district, a zone of transition, working-class housing, middle-class residential, and a commuter zone. Each ring was expected to be distinct in land use, income, and racial composition. Does St. Louis still fit this pattern — or has it fractured it?

Sidewalk Life & Urban Vitality

Jane Jacobs, 1961

Jacobs argued that urban vitality emerges from four conditions: mixed land uses, short blocks, buildings of varying age, and sufficient population density. "Eyes on the street" — the constant informal surveillance of active pedestrian spaces — were her proxy for neighborhood health. This visualization measures vitality through mixed-use density (OpenStreetMap POIs) and workplace concentration (Census LODES).

Black Middle-Class Suburban Identity

Karyn Lacy, Blue-Chip Black, 2007

Lacy documented how middle-class Black suburbanites navigate a precarious position: economically arrived, but still racially marked in predominantly white suburban spaces. They engage in "boundary work" — managing identity through consumption, social networks, and residential choice. This map asks: in St. Louis County, where do middle-class Black households actually live? Are they in the same municipalities as white households at equivalent incomes — or sorted into different spaces?

Geography note: This visualization covers St. Louis County only (FIPS 29189), where suburban municipalities are located. St. Louis city is excluded by design.
